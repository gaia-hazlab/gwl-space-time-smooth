GAIA — Denolle Group Research Agents roster v0.5 · 13 agents · private

A coordinated family of domain-expert agent personas, designed by the scientist, that move a single research project from idea to impact — built on the Olympus governance pattern, renamed to plain research functions, and grounded in the group's three pillars and 8-stage workflow.

This page is both the design rationale and a working review board: read the thinking, then mark each agent Keep / Merge / Cut, leave notes, and Export feedback for the group.

Why build this

A single research project spans literature → ideation → data → method → implementation → results → interpretation → future work. No one person — and no single AI pass — does all eight well. A whole-project skim misses what a focused expert catches, and the maker is a poor judge of their own work. So Gaia splits the work into specialists grounded in the group's own best-practice standards, coordinated by one orchestrator, and checked by an independent auditor and a human at every consequential step. It scales the scientist's scrutiny; it does not replace their judgment.

Lineage: distilled from the Olympus multi-agent system (a private postdoc extraction) and the Earth-science audit, then rebuilt around research instead of software delivery. The valuable inheritance is the governance pattern — separation of powers — not the original roster.

Design philosophy

The scientist sets the Scientific Vision; the agents orchestrate, synthesize, and generate under it. Three Opens feed every agent:

⛁ Open DataAccessible, transparent, reproducible datasets — ingested and QC'd, never silently cleaned.
</> Open SoftwareModular, extensible agents for scientific tasks — swap or improve one without touching the others.
▤ Open KnowledgeScientific literature + numerical methods — the evidence base each persona reasons from.

Separation of powers (the rigor foundation)

  • One hub. The Orchestrator assigns and synthesizes; peers don't call peers.
  • An independent Auditor. Read-only; the maker is never the sole judge of the make, and the Orchestrator can't ship past a Critical finding by fiat.
  • Human gates. Submission, data/code release, field commitments, large compute — a human decides.
  • Design-review first. Plans are audited before resources are spent.

Personas + model tiering

  • Designed by the scientist. Each persona encodes our rigor standards for its slice — not a generic assistant.
  • Right model for the weight. Opus for judgment, Sonnet for building, Haiku for mechanical work — matching the agent to the cost of being wrong.
  • Honest by construction. Capture failures, name uncertainty, disclose AI use, never overclaim.

Merged vs. many smaller agents

The recurring tension (the roster has gone 16 → 10 → 13 → 12 → 13). Neither extreme wins. The test: "would two different people own this in a well-run lab — and do they need to check each other?"

↳ Split into smaller agents

Do it when: independence is required (maker ≠ judge), the cognitive frame differs (derive vs. implement vs. wrangle data), or model tiers differ (mechanical → Haiku).

Pros: focused context, cheap per-task tiering, discrete auditable reports, swap one without breaking others.
Cons: orchestration overhead, context lost across handoffs, boundary disputes, over-fragmentation (a role too small doesn't earn its keep).

⤳ Merge into a broader agent

Do it when: it's genuinely one person's job — a scientist who both writes and engineers code; testing + debugging by one second-pair-of-eyes; the rigor critiques under one judge.

Pros: no handoff friction, one context, simpler mental model, cheaper orchestration.
Cons: diluted focus, lost independence, can't tier per sub-task, one opaque step, a regression in one faculty risks the others.

Gaia's rule: keep the separation-of-powers splits (design / build / judge) no matter what — that's the rigor foundation. Merge only within a side when the work is one craft and splitting adds only handoffs. So: Coder + RSE merged; Test + Debug merged; UQ/V&V/Novelty critiques merged into the Auditor — but Coder, Auditor, and Debugger & Tester stay separate, always.
