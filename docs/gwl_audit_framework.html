§1 Current State Assessment

The existing pipeline is thoughtfully engineered. The NWIS download is checkpointed, the 8-step QC chain is explicit and auditable, and the two-stage spatial baseline + monthly anomaly architecture is architecturally sound. The foundation does not need to be discarded — it needs specific targeted revisions to serve the geohazard applications.

Strengths — keep these NWIS-anchored predictions (every grid cell traces to real well data) · No gap-filling (honest sparse months) · Well-density mask (explicit low-confidence flagging) · QC-rejected datum correction deferred but documented (NGVD29/VERTCON) · Two-stage model architecture (static baseline + monthly anomaly) is the correct decomposition · HUC-2 partitioned variograms handle spatial non-stationarity across CONUS · pixi.toml reproducibility

§2 Scientific Critique of Existing Framework

The current architecture handles the spatial baseline and temporal anomaly interpolation correctly in method, but is missing the physical covariates that would make predictions scientifically grounded, temporally extrapolatable, and embeddable in geohazard models. Four specific gaps drive this assessment.

Gap 1 — DEM is the wrong spatial predictor

Co-kriging with DEM as the secondary variable introduces topographic elevation as the spatial control on groundwater depth. But elevation alone conflates ridges and valley floors at the same altitude. The physically correct terrain predictor is HAND (Height Above Nearest Drainage) — the vertical distance from each cell to the nearest stream channel at the same contributing area. HAND = 0 in valley floors (water table near surface), HAND large on ridge crests (deep water table). MERIT Hydro, already downloaded, contains everything needed to compute HAND. Fan et al. (2013) used a similar landscape-position index as their primary global predictor.

DTW ≈ f(HAND, TWI, slope, contributing area, soil K_sat, geology)
-- not f(elevation) --

TWI = ln(α / tan(β))     -- Beven & Kirkby 1979 topographic wetness index --

Gap 2 — Temporal anomalies have no climate backbone

Kriging monthly anomalies at each time step is a spatial interpolation of what was observed — it cannot extrapolate to months/seasons with sparse data, and it cannot encode why GWL varies: snowmelt, atmospheric rivers, ENSO. For embedding in geohazard scenarios ("what is GWL during a La Niña winter?"), the model needs to be driven by climate indices, not just interpolated from point observations.

The current approach also cannot distinguish the snowmelt-recharge signal in the Cascades from the direct-precipitation response in the lowlands — both appear as a spring GWL rise but with different lag structures and spatial patterns.

Gap 3 — No coastal boundary condition

The SoDo/Duwamish use case involves groundwater within tidal influence: minimum GWL is bounded below by mean sea level plus any tide or surge. Secular sea-level rise (~1.8 mm/yr at Seattle tide gauge) and vertical land motion from post-glacial rebound and engineered fill subsidence (South Lake Union, Harbor Island) create a rising effective baseline that a kriging model cannot capture without an explicit coastal boundary constraint.

Gap 4 — Kriging-only predictions are not smooth at scale

Ordinary kriging with a 300 km search radius produces a smooth field within training data but can introduce bulls-eye artifacts around individual wells with anomalous records, and is discontinuous at HUC-2 boundaries where variogram parameters change. This is precisely the "tiling problem" noted for HydroFrame. The spatial baseline must come from a smoothly-varying regression model, with kriging applied only to residuals.

§3 Proposed Revised Framework

Four stages replace or augment the current three. Stages 1 and 2 are substantial revisions. Stage 3 (kriging of residuals) is unchanged. Stage 4 is new for coastal zones. No numerical flow equations are solved at any stage — this is a simulation driven by observations and climate indices.

Revised Model Architecture — GWL(x, y, t)
STAGE 0
Terrain Derivatives (static, from MERIT Hydro already downloaded) HAND · TWI · slope gradient · contributing area · aspect · stream distance
STAGE 1
Spatial Baseline — Gradient Boosting on Physical Covariates Target: long-term median DTW per well · Features: HAND, TWI, soil Ksat, geology, mean P, mean PET, aridity · Spatial block CV (200 km) · Predict on 1 km grid
STAGE 2
Climate Response Functions — Temporal Signal Per-site OLS: anomaly = β₁·SPI3 + β₂·SWE_anom + β₃·PDO + β₄·AR_count + ε · Interpolate β coefficients spatially · Reconstruct monthly GWL from climate indices at every grid cell
STAGE 3
Residual Kriging — Local Correction (unchanged method) Monthly kriging of (obs − Stage 2 prediction) · Captures local effects not explained by climate drivers · Same variogram approach, HUC-2 partitioned
STAGE 4
Coastal Boundary Condition (coastal cells only, elev < 10 m NAVD88) GWL_min = MHHW + VLM_rate × Δt + SLR_trend × Δt · Sources: PANGA GPS (VLM), Seattle/Tacoma tide gauge (SLR) · Clamp DTW to never exceed this minimum

Stage 0: Terrain Derivatives

Compute from the MERIT Hydro DEM already in data/raw/dem/. No new downloads needed.

HAND(x,y) = elevation(x,y) − elevation(nearest_stream_cell(x,y))
TWI(x,y) = ln(α / tan(β))   -- α = upslope contributing area, β = slope --
slope(x,y) = |∇DEM|          -- from MERIT Hydro native 90 m --

HAND is the dominant physical predictor of GWL depth across all landscape types. TWI adds the convergence/divergence signal. Both are derivable with richdem (already in pixi.toml) or pysheds.

Stage 1: Spatial Baseline via Regression

Replace co-kriging with DEM as a gradient-boosted regression trained on well-observed long-term median DTW. The regression is smooth by construction — no tiling artifacts, no variogram boundary effects. Apply a 200 km spatial block CV (already in the project's modeling reference) to get honest validation metrics.

Current: Co-Kriging (DEM secondary)

  • HUC-2 boundary artifacts
  • DEM elevation ≠ landscape position
  • No soil, geology, or climate covariates
  • Bulls-eye artifacts near anomalous wells
  • Cannot extrapolate to data-sparse regions

Revised: Gradient Boosting (HAND + covariates)

  • Smooth prediction, no boundary effects
  • HAND correctly places valleys and ridges
  • Soil Ksat controls drainage rate
  • Geology type handles karst, alluvial fans
  • Extrapolates via learned physical relationships

Keep kriging — but apply it only to the residuals of the Stage 1 prediction, not to raw WTE. This is regression kriging, which is both smoother and more physically interpretable than co-kriging. The well-density mask still applies.

Stage 2: Climate Response Functions

At each well with ≥ 36 months of record, fit a linear response model relating GWL anomaly to concurrent and lagged climate indices:

ΔDTW(site, t) = β₀
             + β₁·SPI3(t)                -- 3-month standardized precip: drought/flood signal --
             + β₂·ΔSWE(t−1)             -- 1-month lag snowpack anomaly: mountain recharge --
             + β₃·PDO(t)                 -- Pacific Decadal Oscillation: multi-year signal --
             + β₄·AR_count(t)           -- monthly AR landfalls: extreme event pulse --
             + β₅·SPI3(t)×HAND        -- terrain × climate interaction (optional Stage 2b) --
             + ε(t)                       -- local residual → passed to Stage 3 kriging --

Each β coefficient is a site-specific scalar. After fitting at all wells, spatially interpolate (kriging or distance-weighted) the β maps to every grid cell. The final prediction for any cell at any time is then computed analytically from the climate indices — no data needed at inference time beyond the index values.

Why this captures all four PNW phenomena
  • Seasonal (fall storms, spring snowmelt): SPI3 captures the precipitation cycle; ΔSWE at 1-month lag captures the delayed snowmelt recharge in Cascades — β₂ will be large at mountain sites, near-zero in coastal lowlands
  • Extreme events (atmospheric rivers): AR_count is a monthly count of AR landfalls; wells near the coast and river valleys have large β₄, allowing pulse simulation
  • La Niña / El Niño (multi-year): PDO index is the best multi-year predictor for PNW GWL; β₃ captures the wet La Niña / dry El Niño asymmetry observed in NWIS records
  • Coastal SoDo subsidence / SLR: Handled separately in Stage 4 — VLM from PANGA GPS, SLR from Seattle tide gauge

Spatial interpolation of β coefficients

The β maps will be smooth because the underlying physical sensitivity varies slowly with landscape type: mountain wells have high β₂ (snowmelt-sensitive), coastal wells high β₄ (AR-sensitive), valley wells high β₁ (precipitation-responsive). Kriging or IDW of the β values will naturally produce a smooth spatial field — which is precisely what Sanger et al. need for embedding.

Stage 4: Coastal Boundary Condition (SoDo-specific)

For cells where land-surface elevation (NAVD88) < 10 m, constrain the predicted GWL minimum to the local equivalent freshwater head of mean higher-high water (MHHW), adjusted for vertical land motion and sea-level rise:

GWL_min(x,y,t) = MHHW(x,y) + VLM(x,y) × (t − t₀) + SLR × (t − t₀)

VLM(x,y): kriged from PANGA GPS station velocities (mm/yr, ITRF2020)
SLR: +1.8 mm/yr at Seattle (NOAA CO-OPS 9447130) + acceleration term after 2020

§5 Downstream Integration: Sanger et al. & LandLab

Liquefaction (Sanger et al.) — Basin and Valley Focus

  • Output variable: DTW (m below surface) at the engineering event time — e.g., "GWL in SoDo during a La Niña November with an atmospheric river"
  • Spatial smoothness: apply a Gaussian spatial filter (σ = 500 m) to the Stage 1 prediction before passing to Sanger et al. This removes sub-km kriging artifacts while preserving basin-scale variation. Do not smooth the coastal boundary condition.
  • Uncertainty: deliver σ_total = √(σ_kriging² + σ_response²) where σ_response is the residual variance from the climate response fit. Both components needed for probabilistic liquefaction triggering.
  • Scenario generation: the β coefficient maps allow direct scenario generation — feed in a La Niña PDO index, high AR count, and peak SWE to get the "worst case winter" GWL without re-running kriging.
Critical: DTW < 0 check In SoDo specifically, the Stage 4 coastal constraint will physically prevent DTW < 0. For valley floors elsewhere (Duwamish, Puyallup, Snohomish), add an explicit assertion: if Stage 1 + Stage 2 predicts GWL above land surface, clamp to DTW = 0 and flag the cell as "surface-saturated" — that information is itself geotechnically important.

Landslide Hydrology (LandLab) — Mountain Slope Focus

  • Input to LandLab: DTW(x,y,t) passed as water_table__depth on a LandLab RasterModelGrid. LandLab's grid can be upsampled from the 1 km model to whatever resolution the morphodynamic model needs.
  • Saturated fraction: h/H = max(0, (H − DTW)) / H where H comes from a soil depth model (POLARIS dataset, 1 km resolution). Deliver this derived field alongside DTW.
  • Temporal forcing: for LandLab transient stability runs, use monthly GWL time series. The climate response model allows generation of synthetic 50-year scenarios by replaying historical climate indices — needed for probabilistic return-period analysis.
  • Mountain-specific note: the β₂ (SWE) coefficient will be large in Cascade montane zones. The April snowmelt pulse will appear as a sharp DTW decrease in mountain soils — exactly the seasonal saturation that triggers spring landslides. Validate this signal against documented PNW landslide inventories (WA DNR).
Note on LandLab grid compatibility LandLab uses RasterModelGrid (uniform spacing). The 1 km EPSG:5070 output is a uniform raster and can be loaded directly via xarray.open_zarr → numpy array → RasterModelGrid.at_node["variable"]. Ensure the CRS transformation from EPSG:5070 to LandLab's internal Cartesian meters is applied before loading.

§7 Fan 2013 Comparison and Departure

Fan, Li & Miguez-Macho (2013) modeled global water table depth by solving the steady-state Boussinesq equation on a 1 km grid, using topography as the primary boundary condition. The result is physically consistent but static, observation-free, and produces known tiling artifacts — the same problem noted for HydroFrame.

The proposed framework borrows Fan's insight that topographic relief drives GWL patterns (HAND is the same physical principle as the Boussinesq topographic boundary condition) but makes it observation-anchored and temporally resolved: agree with Fan's spatial logic; replace Fan's static solution with a data-driven dynamic one.

Benchmark against Fan 2013 HydroGEN (Ma 2025) is the modern successor to Fan 2013. Both should be kept as spatial pattern benchmarks for the static baseline, but neither should be used as a magnitude prior without EDK bias correction. The 7 m bias in HydroGEN (RMSE 20 m) at PNW wells confirms this.

§8 GAIA Ecosystem Integration

The GAIA HazLab data hub (datahub-inventory) already stages or produces most of the Priority 1 covariates identified in §4, and contains an existing water-table product (Pillar 1) that partially overlaps with gwl-space-time-smooth's goals. Understanding what already exists avoids redundant downloads, aligns on shared formats, and opens the door to Earth2Studio-driven GWL scenario generation.

Positioning: two complementary products, not one Pillar 1 (Soil Reanalysis) provides vadose-zone physics top-down: Richards equation, sub-daily, 30–100 m, fusing DAS seismic dv/v + GNSS + InSAR into a hydromechanical column. gwl-space-time-smooth provides regional GWT observation-anchored bottom-up: NWIS wells, monthly, 1 km, back to 2000. They are complementary — Pillar 1 for daily event-scale vadose dynamics; gwl-space-time-smooth for the decadal GWT signal that sets the initial condition. The two products should cross-validate at the water-table surface.

Stage IV and the Precipitation Intercomparison

The convective-thunderstorms use case (Anderson-Frey, Kharita, Denolle) is building a precipitation product intercomparison that includes Stage IV radar-gauge analysis. This work is directly actionable for the GWL climate response functions (Stage 2):

  • Stage IV (4 km, hourly, 2002–present) captures atmospheric river events at sub-daily resolution — PRISM cannot. Stage IV is the correct precipitation product for fitting the β₄ (AR_count) response coefficient, because the AR signal is sub-daily while PRISM is monthly-averaged.
  • Product intercomparison value: the intercomparison will reveal which product (Stage IV, PRISM, HRRR, CONUS404, ERA5) best predicts GWL anomaly response per terrain zone — use RMSE of {precipitation → observed NWIS anomaly} as the scoring metric. This directly answers which precip product should drive Stage 2.
  • CONUS404 (NCAR 4 km WRF reanalysis, 2000–present) is superior to PRISM for orographic precipitation in the Cascades and Olympics. It is already mentioned in the GAIA pipeline as a soil moisture source — its precipitation field should be evaluated as the primary SPI-3 driver for mountain GWT.
Recommended hand-off The Stage IV intercomparison should produce a ranked table: for each terrain zone (coast · valley · Cascade foothills · mountain), which precipitation product best predicts NWIS GWL anomaly at 1-month lag? Use that ranking to select the Stage 2 precipitation driver per zone — not one product for all of PNW.

Earth2Studio — From Hindcast to Scenario

GAIA already integrates Earth2Studio (GraphCast / AIFS / StormCast 3 km) as the forecast precipitation provider for both the landslide and liquefaction workflows. The GWL climate response functions (§3 Stage 2) make Earth2Studio directly usable for GWL scenario generation without any additional model:

-- Forecast workflow --
1. Earth2Studio (GraphCast/AIFS) → tp field at 0.25° or StormCast 3 km
2. Downscale tp → SPI3(x,y,t_forecast) [PRISM climatology as reference period]
3. Apply β maps: ΔDTW(x,y,t) = β₁·SPI3 + β₂·ΔSWE + β₃·PDO + β₄·AR_count
4. Add Stage 1 baseline + Stage 3 kriging residual (time-invariant at forecast horizon)
5. → GWL forecast (x,y,t+1…+4 weeks) with ensemble spread from Earth2Studio members

-- Same β maps, same pipeline — no new model training required --

Concretely: run Earth2Studio with 50 ensemble members over a La Niña winter forcing, push the precipitation ensemble through the β maps, and read out an ensemble of GWL fields directly embeddable in Sanger et al. or LandLab for probabilistic hazard assessment. The β-map approach is computationally trivial at inference time — matrix multiply, not kriging — making real-time coupling feasible.

Pillar 1 as Cross-Validation Target

Pillar 1's water-table depth output d_wt(x,t) (sub-daily, 30–100 m, from vadose-zone physics) should be compared against gwl-space-time-smooth's monthly 1 km GWT at shared validation sites. This cross-validation:

  • Reveals where the physics model and the observation-anchored model agree (high confidence) vs. disagree (flag for investigation)
  • Identifies wells where Pillar 1's seismic/GNSS signal can correct the NWIS observation (e.g., pumping-influenced wells that kriging incorrectly interpolates)
  • Grounds Pillar 1's absolute depth — it uses Richards equation which requires a lower boundary condition; the NWIS-anchored GWT surface is exactly that boundary
